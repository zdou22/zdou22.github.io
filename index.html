Hello! In this tutorial, we will be analyzing the Montgomery County DOT Parking tickets from July 2021 to July 2022.

Many people these days own cars. One of the worst feelings is parking somewhere and paying the meter, only to forget to pay again and come back to that little piece of paper stuck between your windshield wipers. As someone who regularly drives a car living in Montgomery County, I want to see exactly when people are ticketed the most. If I were to park somewhere and forget to pay, when is it most likely that I'll be ticketed? Let's find out!

1. Data¶

This data was found online at the Montgomery County online database, which is linked here. I have gone ahead and uploaded the data as well, so you can directly get the data.

To get started, we need to download some packages that will help us download the data that we need, as well as clean it up for our using.

What we need right now are pandas and numpy. Pandas is the most commonly used package for data manipulation via it's data structures and operations. Although I do not use numpy heavily in this tutorial, it is very useful for manipulating large arrays and matrices.

2. Data Visualization and Analysis¶

Now that we have our data all cleaned up, we can finally start doing some analysis! First, lets group the dates by the month and see if there is a difference in tickets handed out by month. To create our plot, we can use matplotlib, a package that will help us visualize the data so that we can find trends in the data, that we likely wouldn't be able to tell based off of the dataset alone.

In [160]:
import matplotlib
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

plt.figure(figsize=(20,10))
x = df['Date/Time'].dt.month.unique()
y = df['Date/Time'].dt.month.value_counts()
plt.bar(x,y)
plt.xlabel('Month')
plt.ylabel('Number of Cars')
plt.show()

Now lets try finding out which days of the week are ticketed the most. We can do this similarly to above, but using dt.dayofweek instead of dt.month. With this, Monday is 0 and goes all the way to 6.

In [161]:
plt.figure(figsize=(20,10))
x = df['Date/Time'].dt.dayofweek.unique()
y = df['Date/Time'].dt.dayofweek.value_counts()
plt.bar(x,y)
plt.xlabel('Day of the Week')
plt.ylabel('Number of Cars')
plt.show()

Here, we find something very interesting! There are no tickets given out on Sundays. This is likely due to even metered parking being free on Sundays. However, this doesn't explain why there aren't tickets being handed out for other infractions, such as blocking a fire hydrant. There seems to also be significantly less tickets on Monday, which I would assume to be because as the work week just started, and people typically seem to be less enthusiastic to work on Mondays.

Now lets do the same thing, but with the time.

In [162]:
plt.figure(figsize=(20,10))

Here, we see a trend where there is a high point at 11am, and then consistently high from 5pm to 8pm. This is something that would follow common sense. The morning is steadily rising, as more people start leaving for work, and then dies down while most people are either at work or at school. After this, the continuous highs from 5pm to 8pm are when everyone is out and about in their freetime. With more people comes more tickets.

Below, we want to find out if there is a difference between car brands. Are perhaps DOTS workers more biased to ticket some brands than others? Because there are so many brands, I have limited our dataset so that we exclude models that have very few tickets, given how large our dataset is.

Here, we see Volkswagen leading the pack, with BMW closely behind it. Although I couldn't find a full fledged dataset, I compared it to the most sold cars in the US. On this list, many were Toyota, which is third on our list here. This makes sense as to why Toyota would be third in being ticketed. This was interesting to me, as I could not find a reason behind why Volkswagen was so high up on the list. I also found it very interesting there were so many BMW tickets. This could be due to the fact that Montgomery County has a higher median income, causing there to be more BMWs. The second reason would be because BMW is a more expensive brand, indicating that the owner of the car has a higher salary compared to the average person, meaning they are less worried about ticket costs.
