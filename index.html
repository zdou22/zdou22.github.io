Hello! In this tutorial, we will be analyzing the Montgomery County DOT Parking tickets from July 2021 to July 2022.

Many people these days own cars. One of the worst feelings is parking somewhere and paying the meter, only to forget to pay again and come back to that little piece of paper stuck between your windshield wipers. As someone who regularly drives a car living in Montgomery County, I want to see exactly when people are ticketed the most. If I were to park somewhere and forget to pay, when is it most likely that I'll be ticketed? Let's find out!

2. Data Visualization and Analysis¶

Now that we have our data all cleaned up, we can finally start doing some analysis! First, lets group the dates by the month and see if there is a difference in tickets handed out by month. To create our plot, we can use matplotlib, a package that will help us visualize the data so that we can find trends in the data, that we likely wouldn't be able to tell based off of the dataset alone.

In [344]:
import matplotlib
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

#resize our plot for easy readability
plt.figure(figsize=(20,10))

#Use value_counts to find how many of each unique value we have. Here, it would find how many tickets were in each month
counts = df['Date/Time'].dt.month.value_counts()

#set our x values to be the month
x = counts.index
#set our y values to be the number of tickets per month
y = counts.values

#create a bar graph
plt.bar(x,y)

#label our axes
plt.xlabel('Month')
plt.ylabel('Number of Cars')
plt.show()

From this graph we have made with the months, we see the highest number of tickets coming from June, and the lowest in January. This low point could be due to the cold, where many people are staying inside more. The high in June could possibly be due to school being out, and there is an influx of both high schoolers who can drive, as well as college students coming back from classes out of state.

Now lets try finding out which days of the week are ticketed the most. We can do this similarly to above, but using dt.dayofweek instead of dt.month. With this, Monday is 0 and goes all the way to 6.

Here, we find something very interesting! There are no tickets given out on Sundays. This is likely due to even metered parking being free on Sundays. However, this doesn't explain why there aren't tickets being handed out for other infractions, such as blocking a fire hydrant. There seems to also be significantly less tickets on Saturday. This is could be due to less officers working on Saturdays, resulting in less tickets.

I think that the spike at 11am comes from people forgetting after parking for their job, which slowly dies down as they have already recieved the ticket. Then once people are leaving their jobs and driving elsewhere, this causes the influx of tickets at 6pm.

What if we wanted to compare when people get ticketed differently on an hourly basis, based on the month? For this, we can use a violin plot. This will show us where our data is distributed. For this, we will need to use seaborn. Seaborn is based off of matplotlib, which we were using earlier. However, seaborn is better for creating more indepth visuals.

In [352]:
import seaborn as sns
plt.figure(figsize=(20,10))

x = df['Date/Time'].dt.month

sns.violinplot(x,y)
plt.xlabel('Month')
plt.ylabel('Hour')
Out[352]:
Text(0, 0.5, 'Hour')

From this violin plot, the bulges are where there is a high concentration of data points. From this we can tell that in the months of July to October, the observation we made above where the number of tickets drops off from 2-5pm is most prominent, and the concentration is much more centered around 11am and 6pm. However, something interesting to note is that it is a much more even distribution in the other months. Another point that is interesting is in the coldest months of January and February, there are less tickets at the later hours, which one reason could be because people are staying in more than going out.
